# Biophilic Design Framework for Healthcare Facilities

**Authority:** Biophilic Design principles (Kellert, Browning), applied to healthcare settings  
**Version:** 1.0  
**Last Updated:** 2026-04-05

---

## Purpose

Biophilic design integrates nature into built environments to promote healing, wellbeing, and recovery. For specialist healthcare, aged care, and SDA facilities, biophilic design has evidence-based outcomes on patient recovery, staff retention, and psychological wellbeing.

---

## 14 Elements of Biophilic Design

### 1. Living Systems & Processes
- **Living walls** — vertical gardens throughout facility
- **Green roofs** — photosynthesis, insulation, visual connection
- **Water features** — fountains, ponds, cascades (sound masking, psychological benefit)
- **Responsive plants** — adapt to seasons, engage sensory experience
- **Natural cycles visibility** — day/night, seasons clearly expressed

### 2. Natural Shapes & Forms
- **Fractals & spirals** — natural mathematical patterns (reduces stress)
- **Organic curved walls** — avoid hard right angles where possible
- **Biomorphic architecture** — forms inspired by organisms (not literal copying)
- **Natural proportions** — golden ratio in dimensions, layouts

### 3. Natural Light & Dark
- **Daylighting** — maximise natural light in occupied spaces (minimum 25% of perimeter)
- **Circadian rhythm alignment** — bright light 6am-3pm, dim light 6pm-10pm
- **View corridors** — sight lines to nature from all major spaces
- **Daylight modulation** — control glare without blocking connection (diffusion, louvers)

### 4. Natural Materials
- **Wood** — certified sustainable, visible grain, varied tones
- **Stone** — exposed where appropriate, regional materials preferred
- **Clay** — interior finishes, tactile experience, air quality
- **Natural textiles** — wool, linen, cotton (not synthetics where possible)
- **Avoid plastic/PVC** — use alternatives (cork, linoleum, natural rubber)

### 5. Color from Nature
- **Earth palette** — browns, ochres, greens, blues (not bright artificial)
- **Seasonal variation** — colours that reflect natural changes
- **Colour psychology for healthcare:** Green (healing), Blue (calm), Brown (grounding), Warm neutrals (comfort)
- **Avoid clinical white** — cream, warm neutrals preferred for walls

### 6. Sensory Richness
- **Multi-sensory experience** — engage sight, smell, sound, touch, taste
- **Natural scents** — eucalyptus, lavender, citrus (controlled, not overwhelming)
- **Sound masking** — water features mask stressful sounds (beeps, monitors)
- **Textural variety** — bark, leaves, stone, wood grain, fabric weaves
- **Temperature variation** — warm/cool zones (thermally varied environments)

### 7. Complexity & Mystery
- **Visual depth** — layered landscapes (foreground, middle ground, background)
- **Progressive disclosure** — reveal nature gradually, invite exploration
- **Layered planting** — ground cover, shrubs, trees, canopy
- **Curving pathways** — don't reveal entire layout at once

### 8. Connection to Place
- **Regional materials** — source locally where possible
- **Local ecology** — plant species native to region
- **Cultural plants** — species meaningful to Aboriginal traditional custodians
- **Geological history** — stone types that reflect region's landscape

### 9. Refuge & Prospect
- **Refuge spaces** — intimate alcoves for privacy, contemplation
- **Prospect views** — open sightlines to landscape (long-distance views)
- **Transition zones** — semi-private spaces between public and private
- **Views from retreats** — windows in quiet spaces overlooking nature

### 10. Risk & Reward
- **Appropriate challenge** — activities that engage without overwhelming
- **Small risks** — stepping on stones, climbing gentle slopes (building confidence)
- **Achievable goals** — design supports participant agency and accomplishment
- **Not hazardous** — safety maintained while allowing exploration

### 11. Attraction to Light & Water
- **Water visibility** — views of water features from multiple locations
- **Light quality** — warm light (not harsh fluorescent)
- **Fire equivalent** — focal points that draw attention (fireplaces, candles where safe)
- **Gathering around light/water** — design spaces for social connection

### 12. Exploration & Discovery
- **Trail systems** — walking paths through gardens and nature
- **Varied routes** — multiple ways to reach same destination
- **Seating for observation** — benches, alcoves for nature watching
- **Seasonal change** — different plant interest throughout year

### 13. Sensory Stimulation Modulation
- **Quiet zones** — single sensory input, restorative spaces
- **Varied stimulation** — some areas with rich sensory input
- **Predictable change** — nature changes predictably (seasons, day/night)
- **Avoiding overwhelm** — design allows disengagement from overstimulation

### 14. Embodied Experience
- **Movement through nature** — design promotes walking, gentle activity
- **Physical engagement** — tactile experiences (touching bark, stone, leaves)
- **Postural variety** — standing, sitting, lying, reclining views of nature
- **Comfortable outdoor spaces** — seating, shelter for extended nature exposure

## Healthcare-Specific Evidence-Based Outcomes

| Outcome | Evidence | Implementation |
|---------|----------|-----------------|
| **Pain Reduction** | -37% reduction with nature views (Ulrich 1984) | Ensure patient beds/reclliners have outdoor views |
| **Stress Reduction** | Cortisol levels drop in biophilic spaces | Include green walls, water features in waiting areas |
| **Faster Recovery** | Shorter hospital stays with nature views | Position beds/rooms with garden outlook |
| **Staff Retention** | 8% higher retention in green facilities | Design staff break rooms with plants, views |
| **Sleep Quality** | Improved with natural light exposure & daylighting | Circadian-aligned lighting, nature views from beds |
| **Infection Control** | Green spaces reduce airborne pathogens | Specify infection-control plant species (avoid high allergens) |
| **Mental Health** | Reduced anxiety, depression in nature-rich environments | Accessible gardens for therapeutic use |
| **Attention Restoration** | Nature views restore mental fatigue | Provide views from all major spaces |

## Biophilic Design for Different Facility Types

### NDIS SDA (Specialist Disability Accommodation)
- **Outdoor space:** Accessible gardens with multiple sensory zones (touch, smell, sight)
- **Prospect:** Views from bedrooms & living areas to garden
- **Refuge:** Private nature nooks for respite
- **Texture:** Varied surfaces (stone, bark mulch, decking) for mobility devices

### Aged Care & Step-Down
- **Low-risk engagement:** Gentle slopes, stable stepping stones
- **Seating for observation:** Benches overlooking gardens for social connection
- **Horticultural therapy:** Easy-access raised gardens for therapeutic engagement
- **Proximity:** Nature immediately visible (not distant)

### Hospital Outpatient & VMO Clinics
- **Waiting area nature:** Living walls, indoor plants, water features
- **Window views:** Green views from consultation rooms (reduces anxiety)
- **Natural light:** Maximise daylighting in treatment areas
- **Wayfinding:** Nature-based navigation (garden zones, botanical themes)

### Step-Down Patient Accommodation
- **Balcony/terrace gardens:** Private outdoor space for each resident
- **Therapeutic trails:** Walking paths to support recovery
- **Water elements:** Views of ponds or fountains for contemplation
- **Progressive access:** Gradually increase outdoor engagement as strength improves

## Infection Control Compatibility

**Safe plant species for healthcare:**
- Pothos (air purifying, low allergen)
- Spider plants (toxin removal)
- Dracaena (hardy, low maintenance)
- Sansevieria (air quality)
- Boston fern (humidity, allergen risk if not maintained)

**Avoid:**
- Species requiring frequent pesticides
- High-allergen pollen producers
- Plants prone to mold (poor air circulation)
- Species toxic if ingested
